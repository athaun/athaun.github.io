My Philosophy





Human Centered Software Design

As a software developer and student of software development majoring in computer science, I am always learning new things about the field. I have been a hobbyist of programming and some of the many accompanying disciplines for over five years; I have been conducting research in computational virology for almost three and have been working professionally for under a year. I have been a self-taught student of programming for years and recently started learning under the tutelage of professors in the university environment. I have worked on my own, for an employer, for an advisor, in pairs, in a group and as leader of a team. When designing software, I believe that there are three critical facets to consider, these involve considering the planned users, users’ access, and the design of the software.

Users of software

I believe it is important to consider the people who will be using the software I develop. A website or program can be intended for a specific knowledgeable userbase or for a very general audience. How specific the userbase is determines what features are included, what interfaces are made available and how. While the more general userbases may require a system with less technical jargon, fewer options to get lost in, etc. Each kind of user has different expectations when it comes to how they will use the software and it isn’t possible to develop for every audience.

For example, in web development, it is imperative to keep screen reader usage in mind, to incorporate alt text and navigation shortcuts that make the user’s experience as seamless as possible. In many hobby projects, including in some of my own past websites, accessibility for audiences with disabilities is often ignored. I believe this should change even though it requires effort most people will never realize has taken place.

User Access

Software wields power, and with that power concerns over who has access to what arise. When I develop software, I must consider who should be allowed to have access to the program itself and more importantly, to its source. I have been significantly influenced by the GNU “free as in freedom” philosophy of development, where anybody should have access to a software’s source code. This philosophy of freely open-sourced code has led to great inventions like Linux which has become the backbone of the internet as we know it. With “free as in freedom,” it does not follow that software adhering to the principle is monetarily free, but that the users possess the liberty to view and modify the source code of their software.

Although I have been influenced by the GNU philosophy, I don’t fully believe it is reasonable for every piece of software to allow its users this kind of freedom. It is important for authors to have the option to keep their code as a proprietary creation. A lot of effort goes into designing and development of software, so it is the author’s prerogative to decide the user’s level of access. In certain cases, a client or employer may require that the source be closed and that should be respected. The decision of how accessible to make the source of a program can’t be black and white.

Design of software

One thing I know with certainty is that nobody knows everything there is to know about software development. It is a constantly shifting field of new languages, frameworks, design paradigms and opinions every day. With this in mind, I believe it is silly to exclusively adhere to a single design paradigm. Some people claim nothing can be achieved via object-oriented programming and that data-oriented design is the only way to write code while some people claim the opposite. There are positive aspects to both; in different ways, they both improve the organization of code, however if someone’s code contains a class name like “FactoryBeanConstructerBuilderNode,” then something somewhere has gone terribly wrong.

When writing software, there are a few aspects that are very important to me: readability, efficiency and maintainability. Rather than exclusively applying a single design paradigm, I think that software should be designed for its purpose—where the goal is not to fit into a perfect paradigm but to improve efficiency and to make it as easy as possible for the humans who are reading it to do so, improving maintainability.

Conclusion

I am committed to always learning and improving my skills. I believe in considering the planned users of my software, their access to it, and the overall design of the software. I strive to make my software accessible and easy to use for all intended audiences, and to balance the need for “free”/open-source code with the rights of authors to control access to their work. In my design decisions, I consider the principles of readability, efficiency and maintainability. I also believe in staying open-minded to new design paradigms while avoiding overly complex solutions. My goal is to create high-quality software that meets the needs of its users and clients while considering the author and future maintainers.
